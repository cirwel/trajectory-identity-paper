The most interesting thing is that music turns abstract mathematical structure into felt experience.

Mathematically, music is full of ratios, patterns, symmetry, recursion, and transformation. A simple octave is a 2:1 frequency ratio; a perfect fifth is close to 3:2. Rhythm is arithmetic in time. Harmony is partly about how waveforms overlap. Counterpoint can look like constrained combinatorics. Tuning systems expose deep tradeoffs between pure ratios and equal division of a continuum.

But the strange part is this: we do not experience those structures as equations. We experience them as tension, release, surprise, stability, motion, and emotion.

So the relationship is not merely “music uses math.” It is that music is one of the clearest examples of mathematics becoming perceptual and emotional. It shows that mathematical order is not just something we can reason about; under the right conditions, we can hear it.
